Who Defined It?

Aristotle (384–322 BC): He introduced this structure, particularly in his work Prior Analytics, defining a deductive argument (or syllogism) as "speech (logos) in which, certain things having been supposed, something different from those supposed results of necessity because of their being so". Mathematics Stack Exchange

What Letters Are They (X and Y)?

Aristotle and later logicians used variables or letters to represent the components of a syllogism to evaluate its form. 

The Three Terms (S, P, M): A classic categorical syllogism uses three variables to represent categories:

S: Minor Term (Subject of the conclusion)

P: Major Term (Predicate of the conclusion)

M: Middle Term (Appears in both premises but not the conclusion)

Structure Examples:

Major Premise: All M are P

Minor Premise: All S are M

Conclusion: Therefore, all S are P

The "X and Y" Reference (Maybe): The search results show that in modern descriptions of logic, you might see "Some X are Y" (or M, A, B), but the core letters used in traditional philosophy are S, P, and M.

Letters A, E, I, O: Medieval logicians created mnemonic names for the valid forms using four letters based on the categorical propositions:

A: Universal affirmative ("All...")

E: Universal negative ("No...")

I: Particular affirmative ("Some...")

O: Particular negative ("Some... are not...")

Example: "Barbara" stands for AAA, meaning three universal affirmative premises/conclusion. Wikipedia
